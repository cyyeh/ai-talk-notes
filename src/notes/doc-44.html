Welcome to Our Data Benchmark, Where Everything's Made Up and the Points Don't Matter

Izzy, Hex

✕

This video criticizes the public benchmarks currently used to evaluate "data-analysis AI agents," arguing that most of them don't come close to resembling real data work — so the scores look impressive but are actually pretty meaningless.

Speaker Izzy starts by contrasting some "frontier-style" benchmarks — like having a model rewrite a large program such as SQLite, run a vending-machine company for a year, or OpenAI's browse-comp. These tasks are hard, but at least they're realistic or superhuman in scale, with clear economic value. She then turns to the benchmarks commonly used in data analytics: things like DS-Bench, Spider, Tinybird SQL, Adyen's Dabstep, and Snowflake's DareBench — and points out their problems one by one: questions feel like exams or a translation game (converting overly-specified natural language into SQL), datasets that are largely fabricated or sloppily constructed, scoring that relies on brittle string matching (a decimal vs. a percentage gets marked wrong), questions that are over- or under-specified, unrealistic execution environments (e.g., actually only given a single, pre-selected table), and on top of that, most of them are "single-turn text-to-SQL" with no agent behavior, multi-turn interaction, or long-term memory.

She stresses that this design is nothing like real data work: real work means going back and forth with stakeholders to nail down what "revenue" even means, discovering that Stripe numbers are denominated in cents rather than dollars, dealing with broken ETL, cryptic column meanings, metric definitions that keep getting revised, and iterating for a long time on a messy warehouse. Today's benchmarks just ask "is the answer 47.3 or not," which fails to test the analytical reasoning and practical intuition that actually matter.

She sums up the structural problems with existing evaluations: they're overly focused on "single-turn text-to-SQL syntactic correctness," have too few failure modes, use datasets that are haphazardly cobbled together or only half-heartedly fabricated, rely on strict but brittle deterministic scoring, are largely built by academia without the real product pressure of "climbing the leaderboard and finding the holes," and have both unrealistic execution environments and unrealistic tasks, with almost none of them being agentic in nature. She argues that a small internal eval you use for yourself is fine to be "cheaty" and casual about, as long as it shows relative change — but a public benchmark meant to serve as a shared industry reference needs to be held to a much higher standard.

She then puts forward a core argument: what we actually care about isn't whether a model can get something right "on the first try" — it's whether it can make a mistake in a real environment, learn the lesson, and not repeat it. So evaluation needs to be stateful, long-running, and set in a real, complex environment, rather than treating every question as if the model were dropped into an unfamiliar room and scored on a single item. As an experiment, she took the "failed questions" from some existing benchmarks and placed them in a retryable, memory-enabled environment, giving the model a few more chances and fixing the scorer — and the model ended up solving almost all of them. Under the original benchmark's rules, this would count as "cheating," but in her view, it's actually much closer to how a competent data analyst actually works.

So she introduces her own experimental system, Metric City: a completely fictional company and data warehouse, but with scale and pitfalls deliberately designed to feel very real (millions of rows, nine years of history, platform migrations, renamed columns, inconsistent units, columns only populated during certain periods, dbt marts with deliberately planted bugs, and so on). The whole benchmark is a 90-day simulation: the model is first given a set of about 280 "exam questions" to answer on day 0, and then over the following 90 days, realistic-feeling Jira-style tickets are generated daily, with requests coming from different roles — the model has to query data, write queries, fix pipelines, write notes, update dbt, and even build its own dashboards and alerts. On day 90, it's tested again on the same 280 questions, to see how much the score has improved relative to day 0.

She believes the truly meaningful metric is the "amount of improvement" in the score: many questions are simply unanswerable on day 0, and can only be answered correctly on day 90 after the model has handled the corresponding ticket, fixed the pipeline that needed fixing, and left behind reusable knowledge. This is where you can see the difference in different models' long-term learning ability: stronger frontier models (she mentions GPT‑5 and the Claude family) find it easier to build useful memory and correct past mistakes in this kind of environment, resulting in a steeper score-growth curve. Even so, today's models are still far from "human-level" — a perfect score of 100 is theoretically achievable, but in practice they only reach a little over 70%.

She also demonstrates some interesting properties of Metric City: how a ticket resolution changes future world state (for example, deciding whether to issue a large-scale refund that strains the company's finances — subsequent tickets will reflect that impact), and how a mistaken memory can become a long-term poison — if the model misunderstands a business rule on some earlier day and writes it into the knowledge base, it will keep repeating that mistake every time afterward. This leads her to emphasize that giving an agent memory in a real product is a double-edged sword, and evaluation therefore needs to be able to capture phenomena like "whether a mistake turns into a permanent habit."

She wraps up by circling back to her main thesis: training and evaluation are really part of the same system, and a big part of why models are currently bad at data analysis is that the "carrot" we give them — the benchmark — is designed to be too boring and too far removed from real work, which means even the big labs don't take this domain's shortcomings very seriously. She calls on the industry to build benchmarks that are more realistic, long-running, stateful, and agentic, so that models can be evaluated and advanced in environments that resemble real data work — which is the only way they can truly become reliable "data-analyst colleagues," rather than just answer machines that spit out 47.3 on an exam.
