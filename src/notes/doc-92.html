What It Takes to Build Reliable AI Systems for Production Operations

Steven, Resolve AI

✕

This video discusses how to use AI to build "reliable" production operations systems, particularly for incident response and day-to-day operations.

Steven opens by noting that generating code has gotten cheap (coding agents are very good at writing code), so the real bottleneck has shifted from "writing code" to "operating and debugging after launch." The real production world is noisy, cross-team, and fragmented in tooling, and it's also a high-risk setting — so if you let AI just "act freely" in that environment, the cost of a mistake is enormous (e.g. accidentally deleting a database).

He identifies several core challenges:
engineers spend more and more time handling incidents and debugging customer issues; systems have grown so large that no one fully understands them end to end; the volume of telemetry data is "nearly infinite," so it can't just be dumped into a model; the critical background knowledge mostly lives in people's heads and internal blog posts, and keeps drifting over time; and once AI takes a wrong action in production, the impact is catastrophic, not just a PR comment.

To address these problems, Resolve AI didn't build a single "chatbot" — they built a multi-agent (agent swarm) system:
when an alert fires, it spins up many agents with different responsibilities, like multiple engineers in an incident war room. It first does triage (which services, orgs, clusters are affected, whether it correlates with a specific deploy), then proposes multiple root-cause hypotheses, has them challenge each other and gather evidence, and finally labels out the "root cause," "contributing factors," and theories that were ruled out. Throughout the process it continuously produces a highly readable investigation report, inside which engineers can highlight text, ask follow-up questions, ask the agents to dig deeper into a particular direction, or have it run queries against the telemetry system for them — so they don't have to remember every vendor's PromQL or query syntax.

He spends quite a bit of time discussing why such systems fail, and how to prevent it:
first, models giving "confident but wrong" answers (hallucination). This is especially dangerous in production, because a junior engineer might just act on it directly. So evaluation can't just look at "accuracy rate" — it also has to check whether the model, when uncertain, chooses to admit uncertainty and present its investigation process and supporting data, rather than making things up.
second, the "tribal knowledge" problem: an incident involves many people across many teams, with on-call rotating; if a lot of the investigation process and key insights stay only in that one person's head, the next incident has to start from scratch. An AI system has to simultaneously produce, organize, and maintain org-level knowledge assets (what happened, why, how it was fixed) as a byproduct of helping solve the problem.
third, "not knowing what you don't know": any agent acts on an incomplete understanding. The design needs a good human-AI collaboration interface: the agent has to be able to point out gaps in its own understanding and expose uncertainty in its reasoning so a human can fill it in; and a human also needs an easy way to give the agent instructions and correct its assumptions.

So he argues that AI for production is absolutely not simply "a model problem" — it's a whole-system design problem:
you have to handle integration with all kinds of telemetry and infrastructure tooling, clear behavioral boundaries and permission control, a human-AI communication interface, and — most importantly — evaluation (evals). At Resolve, they "design the eval before designing the system":

  • First, use a positive eval to measure: on incidents where the answer is already known, how often can it reason its way to the correct root cause (roughly, this establishes a baseline of around 30%).

  • Then add a negative eval: measuring how often the model misleads people, and whether it's willing to say "I don't know" when it doesn't.

  • Further design an "evidence chain eval": not just checking whether the final root-cause text is correct, but checking that every step of the entire causal chain is backed by corresponding telemetry or operational evidence — avoiding reward hacking of the "wild guess that happens to be right" kind.

  • Finally, do confidence calibration: labeling each causal link as either "highly causal" or "merely correlated, with some confidence level," so the system can still act reasonably under imperfect information instead of pretending to be omniscient.

On architecture decisions, they also deliberately split "investigation work" from "narrating/reporting to the user," handling them with separate agents, so that a single agent isn't simultaneously trying to reason and write a polished report and getting distracted into mistakes. The permission design for outward-facing actions follows a progressive safety model:
at first, all high-risk actions go through "human-in-the-loop" — the AI can only propose a recommendation and rationale, which an engineer reviews and executes with a button press. Over time, as trust is built up through evals and real incidents, they gradually allow certain safer remediations — like rollback, scale up/down — to run automatically on some low-risk services, with the engineer shifting to "human-on-the-loop," where the focus is monitoring and auditing rather than approving every single action.

He closes by laying out a roughly 12-month roadmap:
in the near term, AI does most of the investigation work while humans approve remediation actions; next, in controlled scenarios, AI is allowed to automatically perform narrow, low-risk fixes; the long-term goal is for AI to do capacity planning and preventive optimization ahead of time, and to become the first-line on-call responder when incidents happen — freeing humans from the pattern of being woken up at 3am and pulling 8 to 30 people into a war room at once. The key to the whole process isn't declaring that it "can be trusted" — it's accumulating evidence and confidence incident by real incident, until its performance in a given domain reliably matches or even exceeds that of human engineers.
