---
title: Building the Blueprint for Scalable Data Products in Public Sector
speaker: Chief Data Officer, City and County of San Francisco
video: https://youtu.be/Kr76S8inySQ?si=j7NlFw6anwg8PaOo
---
This video makes one core point: AI is cheap. What's genuinely hard and critical is data and infrastructure, especially in a high-risk, heavily regulated environment like government and the public sector.

The speaker is the Chief Data Officer of the City and County of San Francisco. She opens with two contrasting examples to show that "it's all AI, but the outcomes can be worlds apart": Taco Bell's AI ordering system malfunctioned (a customer ordered 18,000 cups of water and the system crashed) — it made headlines, but was low-risk. UnitedHealth, by contrast, used AI to make health-insurance payment decisions, and kept denying claims even over doctors' objections, with an error rate as high as 90%, leading to lawsuits and potentially serious harm. She stresses that the problem isn't "AI itself," but the failure of the data and system-governance infrastructure underneath it.

She points out that government today generally suffers from two structural problems. First, data is highly fragmented with no shared definitions: different departments define the same concept (e.g. "veteran") differently, so even though the data exists, it isn't legible and can't be effectively used by systems or AI, and its update cadence carries no SLA to guarantee freshness or correctness. Second, every time a new policy or program launches, it builds its own separate technology stack — its own schema, pipeline, and definitions — disconnected from everything else. In the short term this helps hit deadlines and check the box, but in the long term it makes "evaluating outcomes across departments and programs" nearly impossible, leaving nothing but "innovation theater" with no real value.

She divides government agencies' data maturity into three stages: L1 is reactive — siloed, manual processes, on-prem — where AI basically can't be meaningfully deployed at all. L2 already has a centralized data warehouse and automated pipelines, and the data looks usable, but lacks governance and security genuinely implemented at the technical level, so it "looks ready, but actually isn't safe." L3 is what she calls production-grade: certified datasets, clear data owners, SLAs, and rigorous RBAC permissions and audit trails — only under these conditions can AI operate safely at scale.

She believes the key to crossing from L2 to L3 lies in a handful of concrete decisions and practices. First, establish strong, clear governance and ownership — it doesn't necessarily cost money, but it does require negotiation and consensus, so that every important dataset maps to a clearly accountable owner. Second, implement core definitions (like "veteran") as a single shared piece of code used consistently across all systems, instead of letting every system define it its own way. Third, strict role and permission design, ensuring that "whatever a person can't see, the agent can't see either," with no exceptions. Fourth, invest heavily in unified, reusable data-ingestion and integration patterns — this is the part that consumes the most engineering effort, and also the part most worth investing in.

She also offers a practical "three-question checklist": before letting any AI agent touch data, first confirm — do you actually know what this data is and who owns it? Do you have the legal and policy right to use it? Who can see it, under what circumstances, and is that authorized use rather than just login authentication? And is its freshness good enough to drive real action? If you can't answer any one of these three questions, then in an environment this complex and regulated, you aren't ready to put an agent into production yet.

Finally, she uses San Francisco's own work as a case study: the city's internal data systems used to lack shared infrastructure, standard definitions, and automation. Now her team is building a unified data platform centered on a data lake, establishing certified datasets and a semantic layer so that every department connects to the same platform and avoids duplicating data, and so that new policies and programs are built on this shared stack from day one. Only this way can you genuinely measure a policy's long-term impact across agencies, and only on this foundation can you confidently make use of all kinds of "cool tools" and AI.

She closes with one line: AI is cheap; infrastructure is the hard part. She's currently building out a platform engineering and enablement team, and by strengthening the data and systems foundation, aims to make AI a genuine production system that improves residents' lives — not a fleeting performance.
