Born Different: How AI Natives Build Startups

Panel: Turbopuffer, Cognition, Higgsfield

✕

This video is a conversation between three "AI native" founders and a moderator, on the theme of how startups get "built differently" in the AI era.

To open, the three guests introduce themselves the way they'd "pitch the company to a graduating senior":
Turbopuffer builds an object-storage-based, extremely low-cost vector search engine, aimed at giving AI large-scale semantic search capability, serving Cursor, Notion, Linear, and others. [0:21–1:17]
Cognition builds the software-engineering agent "Devon," with a focus on large-enterprise scenarios such as finance and retail. [1:24–2:04]
Higgsfield focuses on visual understanding and world models, betting that social media content is about to become heavily "synthesized," and aiming to be the leading commercial platform at the front of this wave of image/video AI. [2:13–3:18]

Next they turn to "what's the biggest difference between starting a company in the AI era in 2026 versus five years ago?"
The key word is deep uncertainty. They aren't sure whether AI will end up like the internet or the cloud — just another powerful new technology — or whether it will completely upend how work and roles are divided through self-improvement; they can't even say for sure how many software engineers will still be needed two years from now. [3:39–4:05]
As a result, long-term planning has basically been "torn up and rewritten": instead of one-year or six-month plans, they now work off a one-month or even one-week horizon, reshuffling strategy monthly or even weekly when necessary. [4:20–4:37][4:38–5:03]

In an environment like this, "clarity and urgency" instead come from short-cycle, well-defined plans. Cognition's approach: accept that next month is uncertain, but this month's plan has to be very clear, and it has to "absolutely get done." Plans are usually drafted first within a small circle, then opened up for the team's input, and finalized last, on a roughly weekly cycle. [5:17–6:16]

Turbopuffer, by contrast, chooses to heavily "delegate" decisions: any engineer can directly add credits for a customer to clear a blocker, without layers of approval. [7:01–7:20] He compares the organization to a set of "models," where management's job becomes "updating everyone's weights" so all their vectors point in the same direction; as long as 80-90% of the direction is right, the rest gets corrected together after the fact. [7:22–7:53]

On "using AI to pick hires," what Higgsfield especially values is whether a candidate has genuinely used AI to make themselves 10x — whether they can describe a specific, concrete situation, rather than vaguely saying "I've used such-and-such tool." He's found that even in Silicon Valley, engineers' AI adoption is way out ahead, but plenty of business roles haven't caught up at all. [7:59–9:05]

Looking ahead, he thinks traditional industries (accounting, law, etc.) will become heavily AI-driven by the end of this decade, and many B2B SaaS products (Salesforce, HubSpot, etc.) will turn into "headless databases," with a new AI agent layer growing on top — though exactly what interface these professionals will end up working in is still unclear. [9:10–10:03]

On management and organizational structure, the panelists hold rather different views.
Higgsfield's take: the AI field leans heavily on "spiky talents" (people who are extremely strong along one dimension), and such people are sometimes uneven in interpersonal skills or communication, which makes "good talent management" matter even more; but multi-layered organizations start to look inefficient once AI helps relay and organize context, so managers need to be able to take on more direct reports and learn to use AI for spreading context. [10:28–11:43]

Cognition's strategy is to organize "around product surfaces" rather than traditional functional divisions: for instance, the Devon web team, the Windsor IDE team — each team is like a mini-startup, owning its slice of the product end to end. Managers are simultaneously people managers and GM/product owners. [13:25–14:07] They feel 5-10 people is the ideal team size. [14:18–14:32]

Turbopuffer currently has only 15 engineers, and feels the opposite pressure: since AI lets a small team produce what used to take a dozen-plus people, they lean toward 2-4-person or even single-person squads, and the bottleneck instead becomes "how fast humans can understand the problem." The upside is that managers get to have much deeper interaction and coaching with each person. [14:36–15:51]

On "how big should the whole company eventually get," they don't really set a hard ceiling — instead they keep asking "how do we spread out decision-making and avoid big meetings," trying to keep meetings under 6 people, and using recordings, transcripts, and AI summarization tools to help people catch up afterward. [16:14–17:07]

On "context sharing," Cognition has an interesting ritual: the whole engineering team (roughly 40 people) does a joint standup twice a week, with each squad taking turns reporting what they've recently shipped, what they're working on now, and where they're stuck. [18:40–19:19] This gives everyone a shared feel for the overall rhythm. Some teams have tried having their own agent, Devon, auto-compile these updates from Slack — sometimes it works great, sometimes it turns into noise; they've noticed that once the AI summary quality isn't good enough, people simply stop reading it. [21:38–22:20]

Because Turbopuffer is remote-first, the company built a high-intensity, asynchronous Slack culture from day one. As message volume has grown, they're considering grouping communication into "shards" and having AI help summarize each shard. [20:15–20:40] There's also a very popular "shipped-it" channel: whenever anyone produces any result at all — from fixing a dashboard to shipping a major feature — it gets posted there, so every department can share in the momentum together. [20:47–21:21]

The moderator also points out: quite a few companies have tried using AI to help everyone "see what the company is up to," but after experimenting they often find it doesn't work well — plain old human-written updates still end up more useful. [22:21–22:33]

On performance management, Higgsfield feels "who is 10x" is easier to spot than it used to be: once everyone has AI, the people who genuinely turn AI into 10x leverage become very obvious. [22:53–23:10] What's actually hard is "letting people who don't fit go," and, with the overall pace pushed higher by AI, figuring out how to help roles that can't keep up.
He has an interesting framework: building a billion-dollar company probably takes only about ten truly high-efficiency, AI-savvy key people. But to reach hundred-billion-dollar scale (Stripe-level), you need at least 80 such highly aligned core members who deeply understand both the market and the technology. [23:35–24:07][24:32–25:12] And this core doesn't necessarily have to come only from early employees — plenty of "juice concentrate" people can be brought in during the mid-to-late stages too, it just takes effort to get them up to speed on context. [25:32–26:07]

Cognition's observation: early employees' advantage lies in being deeply familiar with the company's history and cross-cutting context, but as the company scales, making a new hire successful no longer really requires "knowing everything" — having enough depth in their own line of business is enough. [26:11–27:07]

On "competing for talent against giant labs like OpenAI," Turbopuffer's edge is being remote, letting them pull in extremely strong engineers who don't want to, or can't, move to San Francisco. They've also deliberately shaped their brand voice to appeal to "P99 engineers": the site has a retro-hacker look, ASCII art, and architecture diagrams right on the homepage, plus an intuitive pricing calculator and performance benchmarks — so that anyone who truly understands search sees it and thinks "these people get what I'm dealing with." [27:27–29:07]

Cognition instead offers "something closer to what joining OpenAI felt like three years ago": joining at a stage where you still have huge influence and scope, rather than a company that's already gone very bureaucratic or massive. They put a lot of weight on trial work, having candidates actually work alongside the team for a few days to experience a high-density, do-more-talk-less culture. [29:24–31:38] Team value isn't about "saying clever things" — it's about "what you've actually shipped."

The later part turns to "yap vs. ship": how much "speaking up, marketing" counts as the right amount? Cognition admits that after Devon initially went viral, they actually under-invested in content output for a long stretch, and now feel they should "talk" a bit more, especially for recruiting and educating the market. [32:11–32:53] Turbopuffer's CTO adds: at the individual level you can yap less and ship more, but at the company level you still need a presence in this era.

Higgsfield uses "fully AI-generated feature films" as an example: for a long time people talked up this dream and put out a lot of hype, but the technology couldn't keep up for a while; now they're genuinely about to announce a fully AI-generated film at Cannes, finally giving the past "yap" enough "substance" to back it up. [33:17–34:12] He also stresses: lively AI discussion on X/Twitter doesn't mean AI use is widespread globally — most people are actually still way behind, which is why marketing and education still matter enormously. [34:18–34:43]

Finally, they discuss "how a small startup works with companies far bigger than itself."
Higgsfield prefers partnering with smaller, agile creator teams and DTC brands, using real results to prove how AI improves efficiency and business models, while keeping a "democratizing" narrative and avoiding big-platform-style lock-in tactics. [34:59–36:02]
Turbopuffer points out that breaking into large companies comes down to solving a real pain point "that they can't solve themselves," paired with extreme customer success, so you become an extension of their team. [36:03–37:00]
Cognition's first big enterprise customer involved flying the whole company on-site for a week, aggressively "canvassing floor by floor" to find the key internal people, doing everything possible to guarantee a successful rollout. As they've scaled, they've also noticed that AI-native companies have a very different engineering-to-sales headcount ratio than the last generation of SaaS: 40 engineers, but sales headcount is already at 200, because enterprise customers need to be genuinely "walked through it" before product value turns into realized value. [37:05–38:14]

They also emphasize "everyone is GTM" as a cultural value, wanting engineering and sales to function as one whole rather than two siloed departments; for example, they require every engineer to visit a large enterprise customer's site at least once, to see firsthand the reality of "one change touching 50 repos, tens of thousands of codebases" — only then will they truly understand how the product ought to be designed. [38:24–40:43]

At the very end, each speaker gives a shout-out to a colleague who stood out that week — from someone who single-handedly carried the entire full-text search line, to someone with a major breakthrough in static-data research, to the project lead who drove the company's biggest launch — circling back to the opening idea that "AI lets small teams produce enormous output, but in the end, it's still people driving everything." [40:45–41:36]

Overall, this conversation paints a typical picture of "AI-native startups":
Small teams, dense concentrations of talent, extremely short planning cycles;
Decision-making and responsibility heavily delegated, held together by culture and metaphor (like "everyone is a model");
AI used to amplify individual output, and also used to cut down organizational layers and meetings;
all while, out in the wider world, having to compete with giant labs for talent and educate mainstream industries technically and mentally at the same time.
