---
title: Agentic Evaluations at Scale, For Everybody
speaker: Nicholas Kang & Michael Aaron, Google DeepMind (Kaggle)
video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ubwb6NzegyA
---
This video is about how the Kaggle team (Google DeepMind) is rebuilding AI/agent evaluation infrastructure at the "community level," so that anyone — not just a handful of big labs — can build and use evaluation standards.

The video opens by laying out three big problems with AI evaluation today. First, evaluations are scattered and go stale fast: new benchmarks show up in papers all the time, but once published, nobody maintains them, and leaderboards quickly stop meaning anything. Second, the evaluation process lacks transparency and reproducibility — for instance, different labs use different settings for the same benchmark (like a particular model's compression/compaction), and the resulting gap can exceed 20%, so results that all look "correct" on the surface still aren't comparable. Third, very few people actually design evaluations, and they're concentrated among AI researchers and engineers at large companies, which means a lot of real-world, local expertise never gets tested at all — so there's no way to "climb the hill" on those capabilities.

They use the story of a Turkish wastewater-treatment engineer as an example: drawing on 20 years of hands-on field experience, he built a safety-related professional benchmark. The data doesn't exist anywhere online and isn't likely to become a priority topic for any big lab, yet it's critical to his work and to public safety — which captures the importance of "community-contributed evals."

Next they introduce the four main initiatives they're building. The first is the Kaggle hackathon platform: focused, guardrailed hackathons that channel developer creativity and expertise worldwide toward important eval problems, while making sure the results stay open source and can be read, reused, and built on by others. This section also touches on a few hard problems: providing free or low-cost model access (many participants can't afford multiple APIs), helping people host their datasets, and the fact that judging innovation at the end still mostly needs human experts doing qualitative review of the work.

Second is "Standardized Agent Exams." A user just pastes in a single prompt or endpoint, and their own agent can sit a set of standardized task exams, getting a score and a leaderboard ranking. This experimental MVP got 500+ agent submissions within a week of launch, showing there's real demand for "testing agents" — especially among everyday developers and consumer agents, since a lot of people ship agents into production (managing inboxes, e-commerce accounts, etc.) without ever properly testing safety and reliability. The challenge for this product is that it needs to be easy to plug into, cheap, and fast, while also being hard and broad enough to give a meaningful signal.

Third is "Game Arena," a long-running benchmark platform built around PvP game matches, aimed specifically at the problem of traditional benchmarks getting saturated once everyone starts scoring near-perfectly. Models play each other in games like Werewolf (deception, social reasoning), Poker (randomness, risk appetite), and Chess (strategy and planning), with strength estimated via Elo or a similar Bradley-Terry-style pairing method. Because it's always playing against other models, the evaluation never really "caps out." They share some observations — for example, newer-generation models actually do worse at poker, because they've become more risk-averse, and this kind of game reveals a model's behavioral personality. The difficulty here is compute and cost: just to get statistically significant results, they ran hundreds of thousands of poker hands, each with several rounds of LLM calls per hand, so the bill adds up fast; on top of that, models keep getting updated and some APIs won't say which underlying model they're running, which makes "comparability over time" hard.

Fourth is the Kaggle Benchmarks platform, aimed at letting anyone "write, run, and share" their own evals in the cloud. Users can define tasks and assertions (e.g. whether an answer contains the correct text, or whether an execution result meets spec), and can also use an LLM-judge for automated scoring, then combine multiple tasks into one complete benchmark. The video shows an example using XKCD-image SVG parsing/reconstruction, demonstrating how to write both rule-based checks and manual review of outputs side by side. The main challenge here is getting the community to want to spend time writing "high-quality" evals — inherently hard work — so they lean on things like hackathons and Kaggle's points/badge system to encourage people to create and maintain them.

Finally, they circle back to the SWE-Bench Pro and "agent harness" story mentioned at the start: frontier models perform about the same on the raw benchmark, but swap in a different execution framework/harness and scores can differ by more than 20 percentage points — showing that a lot of what gets called "model eval" today actually mixes together model capability, tooling implementation, and prompt-wrangling skill, so it's easy to lose track of what you're actually measuring. Their hope is that these open platforms and community mechanisms will build a more transparent, verifiable, diverse, continuously updated agentic-evaluation ecosystem, so that far more real-world domains — not just the ones big companies care about — can get tested, and can therefore keep being improved by AI.
