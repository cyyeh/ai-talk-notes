From Postgres to ClickHouse and Back: Building a Unified OLTP + OLAP Database for AI Workloads

Kaushik, ClickHouse (PeerDB)

✕

This video explains why "Postgres for transactions + ClickHouse for analytics" is becoming a general-purpose architecture, and how the ClickHouse team is making this path viable and operable.

Speaker Kaushik starts with his own background: in 2023 he built PeerDB, aiming to make "moving data out of Postgres" simple, syncing data to various queues, storage systems, and OLAP engines (Snowflake, BigQuery, ClickHouse). In 2024, PeerDB was acquired by ClickHouse, because they were seeing so many customers piping Postgres data into ClickHouse — and not as a one-time migration, but as a long-term "use both together" pattern.

He first positions the two systems briefly: Postgres is today's most general-purpose, mainstream OLTP database, well-suited to row-based queries and transactions, with extensions and strong ACID guarantees; ClickHouse is a columnar OLAP system, used for real-time analytics, roll‑ups, and high-volume ingestion, trading off some transactional consistency for high throughput and query speed. Both lean open-source-first, but they serve clearly different purposes.

He then describes the pattern he's observed: from May 2024 to now, the number of customers using the "Postgres + ClickHouse" pattern has grown roughly 100x, with over 500 TB of data flowing between the two every month — and none of this is "migrate and cut over," it's long-term coexistence. This pattern shows up across many different industries, not just a single vertical.

He identifies three typical company scenarios. The first is "AI-native companies," where AI is the core product itself, not just a bolt-on feature. These companies generate huge volumes of model calls, tool calls, and event tracking, and their data grows extremely fast — he did a rough analysis of companies with "ai" in their domain name and found their data volume grows by an average of 1000% within 6 months. A traditional SaaS company might not hit an analytics bottleneck until it reaches Series B/C with Postgres data in the 100–500 GB range; AI companies hit the wall in just 3–6 months, forcing them to adopt ClickHouse very early — sometimes needing to plan for it from day one.

The second is "security and compliance" companies, which need to ingest huge event streams. On one hand, they need real-time anomaly detection and alerting over massive amounts of data; on the other, they must strictly log every row‑level event (e.g., IAM policy changes, IP changes), requiring strong transactional guarantees and high-throughput analytics side by side. This log/data pattern closely resembles clickstream/ad-tech systems, where ClickHouse is especially well-suited for analytics while Postgres holds the critical transactional state.

The third is B2B product companies that "need to provide analytics on top of their own business data" — for example, restaurant POS systems (he cites SpotOn): Postgres handles orders, billing, and transactions, while ClickHouse handles analytics and reporting like foot traffic and customer counts. These are typically companies that have grown to a certain scale, with the classic dual need of "online transactions plus reporting/analytics."

Having established how widespread this pattern already is, he turns to the "pain points": Postgres + ClickHouse sounds elegant, but the actual architecture is often very complex. He shows Instacart's public architecture diagram: there's a pile of components in the middle, including Kafka, and operating these systems requires a dedicated team on its own — and if any single link breaks, the whole data sync goes wrong. There are two hard problems: first, moving data and keeping both sides in sync needs to stay reliable under heavy traffic; second, the application itself needs to be rewritten to query ClickHouse, which is typically months of engineering work on its own.

To solve these problems, ClickHouse launched its own managed Postgres service, aiming to "control both the source and the destination" — which is the only way to achieve a high-quality, low-latency two-way integration. The reference architecture roughly looks like this: Postgres runs on NVMe, optimizing for low tail latency; ClickHouse stores data on object storage like S3/GCS, optimizing for high throughput; in between, a CDC mechanism keeps the two sides efficiently in sync without depending on a pile of third-party components, paired with a layer that makes application migration easier (PG ClickHouse).

On the CDC (Change Data Capture) side, he says he's "spent basically the last three years doing exactly this," and summarizes a few key capabilities. First, large-scale backfill: when you have 10–100 TB of historical data sitting in Aurora/RDS that needs to move to ClickHouse, using ‎⁠pg_dump/pg_restore⁠ would be painfully slow, so instead they do "consistent parallel snapshotting" — splitting the snapshot up and moving it in parallel, so a backfill at the hundred-TB scale can finish in days instead of months. Second, controlling the load on the replication slot: CDC can easily make the source Postgres fragile, so they continuously consume the slot to keep lag very low, even decoding in‑flight transactions before they commit, paired with a state machine that ensures the ClickHouse side can roll back when needed, reducing pressure on the OLTP system. Third, moving WAL decoding off to the side: WAL decoding itself is heavy on CPU/IO, so they ship the WAL to S3 or another object store first, then use an external process to decode and ingest it, leaving the live Postgres free to focus on transactions. The overall goal is end‑to‑end latency of under 1 second, from an event being written to Postgres to being queryable in ClickHouse.

The second big problem is "application migration." Even once the backend has ClickHouse for analytics, having every service manually rewrite its queries to hit ClickHouse is still a long project for a large company. To address this, they built an Apache 2-licensed open-source Postgres extension called PG ClickHouse. This extension intercepts queries against Postgres, analyzes which parts can be pushed down to ClickHouse, and acts as an FDW (foreign data wrapper), pushing expensive computation over to ClickHouse for execution. He mentions that it can currently push down 18 of the 22 TPCH queries — it won't necessarily support every query, but most queries and joins already work, and it can even push down sub-plans. From the application's perspective, the experience becomes "just swap the schema for the corresponding PG ClickHouse schema, and queries automatically get pushed to ClickHouse" — ultimately, you can treat ClickHouse as a "read replica" sitting on top of Postgres, dedicated to handling analytical queries.

In summary, he argues that if the industry's pursuit of "HTAP / unified OLTP + OLAP" means building a brand-new database from scratch, it inevitably comes with new trade‑offs — and you usually only discover those problems once you're halfway through migrating. By contrast, pairing Postgres (which already represents transactional best practice) with ClickHouse (which represents analytical best practice), and then turning the two hard problems — data sync and application migration — into a product-grade experience, gives you a much better shot at maintaining world-class performance on both the transactional and analytical sides at once. At the end of the video, he frames this whole approach as a blueprint for a "unified database stack": developers get a single, easy-to-use experience at the application layer, instead of having to cobble together a pile of systems themselves.
