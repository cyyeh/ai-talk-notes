How Product and Research Build Together at the Frontier

Izzy & Olivia, Hex

✕

This Hex conversation at AI Council mainly shares their hands-on playbook for "building product and research at the frontier," rather than demoing features themselves.

What is this video about?

Izzy is Hex's Head of AI Research, and Olivia is the lead for core product. The two of them talk through how to plan a product when models get a new version every few weeks, when to "push forward" and when to "stop," and how research and product pull against each other while also enabling each other.

  1. How each of them arrived at their current role

Izzy originally worked in marketing/developer relations at Hex, not a typical engineering background. His career-shift story is that a deep understanding of users and product, combined with models (like Claude Sonnet) getting more capable, let him become a highly productive engineer and researcher with LLM assistance. He stresses that the real key is "pathological curiosity and persistence," not a traditional résumé.

Olivia joined Hex as an engineer and was initially responsible for the first version of its AI features, back when it ran on GPT‑3.5 and performed poorly. Since there was no PM, she stepped up to do the job herself, and because she carried no traditional PM baggage, she was able to redefine the PM role starting directly from the question "as an engineer, what do I need most right now?"

  1. The first 80% is easy — the real difficulty is the 80→95% "quality gap"

A point they kept returning to: in the LLM era, "building something 80% of the way to a demoable state" has become very easy, but polishing it into something genuinely stable and reliable (e.g., a 90%+ success rate, ready for production with large numbers of users) — that last 20% is extremely hard, and full of strange, mutual interference between parts.

For instance, Hex's analytics agent has more than 70 tools, plus various memory systems, guides, example queries, and so on. Every additional feature subtly changes the model's behavior: for example, seeing an example of a past query, the model confidently YOLOs a query against ‎⁠dim_customers⁠ without even checking the schema, then guesses at column names. This kind of "looks smarter but is actually less stable" side effect is, they say, something they deal with on a daily basis.

  1. What counts as "research"? What's merely "engineering"?

Izzy splits Hex's "research work" into two categories:

One category is things that are "fuzzy in quality yet critically important" — usually not turned directly into a standalone product feature, but which profoundly shape the overall experience. Examples include using an LLM to do claim validation, or having one LLM judge whether another LLM's SQL/data analysis is reasonable (even without a ground-truth answer).

The other category is "new problems nobody in the world has truly solved yet" — for example, genuinely reliable automatic evaluation of data work, reference-free eval, or automatic sanity checks on SQL query results. These aren't the kind of problems you solve by putting on some binaural-beats YouTube video and firing off a single prompt; they require a whole cycle of roadmapping, repeated experimentation, pushing forward, and rolling back.

By contrast, take a feature like "Generative apps": you could already just ask Claude to write you a JS app — it's technically already proven feasible. Hex's value-add is more about turning that into a workflow that's safe, permission-controlled, integrated into Hex, and easy to use. He considers this to lean toward engineering rather than research.

  1. How long to "grind it out," and when to admit: just wait for the model

This is one of the areas where they admit they stumbled the most. Two years ago, they already wanted to build the notebook agent they have today, but the models back then weren't good enough. They spent a lot of time "grinding it out," desperately building all sorts of complex agent systems and research projects — which, in hindsight, was like trying to squeeze blood from a stone.

Their conclusion: you can't always know upfront that "the next generation of models will solve this," but you need to learn to call it quits early. When you find that piling on hack after hack still can't get you past a certain ceiling, it's likely that the model itself just isn't there yet — and at that point, a better move is to step back and build "infrastructure that will definitely be useful once the model improves," instead of continuing to dig yourself deeper.

Through their relationships with Anthropic and OpenAI, they work backward from "questions those labs can't answer" to figure out their experimental direction — trying to guess which capabilities are about to be absorbed into the models, and which gaps won't be filled anytime soon — and use that to decide which problems are worth investing their own high-cost, ambiguous research into.

  1. A classic painful case: the UUID aliasing system and lessons in decomposability

One concrete story: in the early notebook agent, the model simply couldn't remember or correctly type out long UUIDs, and Hex relied heavily on UUIDs for permission control. To make the agent usable, they built an entire ref registry: mapping complex UUIDs to short IDs, so the model only ever interacted with short IDs, while the system handled the conversion and validation.

At the time, this was the critical piece of engineering that let the product "survive." The problem is, they built this piece to be too "foundational" and too "long-lived," coupling it heavily into the entire agent harness. By the time models like Claude / Opus 4.5 had become 99% accurate at handling long IDs — making the registry nearly unnecessary — ripping it out turned out to be extremely painful, becoming a huge operational and development burden.

Their reflection: a lot of infrastructure built to compensate for model shortcomings is, by nature, a "transitional system," and should be designed from day one to be "removable and disposable," rather than treated as a permanent core.

  1. Product vs. research: when should you "just ship it to users"?

Olivia's philosophy leans toward "if the net benefit is clear, ship it" — even with bugs and an imperfect success rate, as long as it's genuinely 10x faster or 10x better on certain workflows, many customers are willing to accept rough edges.

One personal standard she holds herself to as a PM is: "if I removed this feature today, would it actually hurt?" If yes, then it's probably good enough to ship. She also emphasizes that a new responsibility for PMs is to "preserve room for experimentation" for the team, while also having the courage to call it done once something is "good enough," rather than getting dragged down chasing "almost perfect."

Izzy, on the other hand, often wants to spend an extra two to four weeks on a research spike to substantially raise a feature's quality. Their observation is that whenever they deliberately pause to do this kind of spike, it almost always "thoroughly upgrades" the initial version; conversely, if they never set aside this kind of research time, they end up accumulating a large pile of things over the long run that are "usable but not good enough."

The biggest tension between the two of them is knowing when to defer to "research instinct" and when to defer to "let customers use it and fix things as you go." What they rely on is internal eval infrastructure, real-world error-rate data, and alpha testing with the right customers to decide which problems are worth continuing to dig into.

  1. Eval, data work, and the difficulty of "healthy skepticism"

Izzy spends a fair amount of time discussing what makes data work especially hard: it looks verifiable (data has right and wrong answers, after all), but in practice it's highly subjective and steeped in domain knowledge.

He gives an example: he deliberately planted a fanout bug in their internal AE performance dashboard, making every AE appear to have a quota attainment rate in the thousands of percent. Ask any frontier model "how did this quarter's performance look?" and it will excitedly declare: amazing, best quarter ever. Only when you follow up with "this number looks off, could it be a data quality issue?" will it immediately spot the bug, apologize, and correct it.

In other words: the model has zero instinct to "doubt the number itself" unless a human doubts it first. How to cultivate the right kind of healthy skepticism, without turning the model into something that "doubts everything and believes nothing," is the eval/claim-validation research problem he's currently working on.

  1. The collapse and rebuilding of the roadmap in the LLM era

Olivia says that in a world where models get major updates every few weeks, "traditional roadmapping barely exists anymore." A 12-month roadmap you write today could have all its priorities completely reshuffled just a few weeks later when a new model ships.

Their practical approach is:

  • Fewer features are truly "hard-committed" on the roadmap — most of what remains is foundational, must-do work like security and stability.

  • They openly state that the roadmap will change, setting expectations upfront in internal communication.

  • They leave engineers room for spontaneous ideas and prototyping — many important features (like user memory) started as prototypes engineers built on their own initiative, which then forced their way onto the roadmap.

On the research side, they keep their long-term focus on a handful of "white whale"-level problems: eval, data trustworthiness, long-horizon agents, enterprise data workflows, and so on — treating "which big problems are we solving" as a more stable north star than "what feature ships next Q3."

  1. Coopetition and differentiation with frontier labs

On the question of "when Anthropic/OpenAI start building products themselves, what moat do you have left?" their answer is: if you never think about this question at all, you're basically waiting to die; but at the same time, you can't view it purely through a "feature-for-feature competitor" lens either.

They see their advantage as falling into two parts:

One is infrastructure and workflow. For example, Hex had sandbox environments and programmable cloud execution environments early on, which suddenly became super-critical "infrastructure" in the agent era, letting them directly leverage advances in frontier models.

The other is deep understanding of specific workflows. Much of what makes data work genuinely hard happens around a company's private internal data and complex business logic — none of which shows up in public training data. Model companies are unlikely to, and don't have the time to, polish every single vertical workflow to perfection — which leaves room for teams like Hex that focus specifically on "data workflows."

The video closes not with "we already have the complete answer," but by sharing the scars and heuristics they've picked up from over a year of operating at the frontier: learning to call it quits early, designing systems to be removable from the start, accepting the fluidity of the roadmap, swinging back and forth between research and delivery, and being willing to kill off old features they worked hard to grow, in order to make room for new experiences driven by more capable models.
