5 Lessons from the Classroom for Evaluating Agents

Andrew Zigler, Dev Interrupted

✕

This talk is about carrying the mindset of classroom teaching and student assessment over into designing and evaluating AI agents.

Speaker Andrew Zigler opens by sharing two failed hackathon experiences. The first was a sensor-based game app: he and his teammates each used different agents and tools (frontend, backend) to build at high speed, but in the end nothing integrated cleanly — the demo could only show the frontend, with the backend never wired up. The second was a data-science agent hackathon: faced with a sprawling mix of structured and unstructured documents, he tried to "brute-force control" the LLM with rules and notes, and ended up with a mediocre result and a messy repo. Afterward, he asked Cursor "why did I lose?" and it told him he'd been "fighting the LLM" instead of "teaching it" — which made him realize he'd just kept piling on rules without ever really demonstrating and teaching the model what a good answer looked like.

He then brings his teaching experience into it. The first analogy is the classroom calendar / the student's homework planner: writing down daily learning goals and assignments is one of the earliest and strongest predictors of long-term learning outcomes. The same holds for agents — relying solely on short-term memory or chat logs isn't enough. What's needed is a durable, shareable, structured "agenda/plan" that lets humans and multiple agents stay aligned on context, instead of everyone charging off in their own direction.

This leads to the key tooling concept: Steve Yegge's Gastown and its core primitive, "beads." Beads are essentially a pile of JSON nodes — each with an id, title, description, notes, and so on — that can be linked together into a graph (dependencies, epics, subtasks), somewhat like "structured Jira tickets." The biggest difference is that for an agent, writing a bead is just a lightweight tool call (push/pull) — there's no need to read an entire file first to decide how to change it, which avoids the friction of markdown notes, where you have to read the whole file every time and risk polluting context. Beads thus become what he calls the "agenda": a task graph that's readable and actionable by both humans and agents.

He walked through his own later hackathon workflow: at The Atlantic's competition, instead of diving straight into code, he spent several hours talking to his computer (using Whisper for voice input) to describe in detail the product he wanted to build — turning Atlantic articles into a virtual-classroom system where teachers could assemble courses from articles, and students could read, highlight, write essays that synthesize multiple sources, and be pushed toward correct citation. He first organized everything — the competition rules, the judges' preferences, his own teaching expertise, and his product vision — into reference material, then worked with the agent to break all of it down into a large number of small, interlinked bead tasks. Once "class was in session" and the bead graph was built, he kicked off Claude Code to let the agents implement automatically from those beads, while he went to lunch and helped other teams. When he came back, most of the functionality was already in place on the first pass, and he only had to handle some UI details and demo data. In the end he took first place at the hackathon, having hand-written barely any code.

This workflow made him realize that as long as you clearly teach, up front, what a good outcome looks like and break it into structured tasks, agents can operate highly autonomously through the middle and later stages. It's really the same as what he does in the classroom: teachers start with "backward design" — first defining what students should know by the end of the term and what the final exam looks like — and only then design the teaching activities. In engineering terms, that's TDD thinking.

He then pushes a step further: how to systematically evaluate agent performance. Traditionally, a lot of people like to build a spider chart with five dimensions each scored 0–5 (helpfulness, truthfulness, tone, and so on), but he cites Hammel's view that such dashboards are of little use for optimization, because the difference between a 4.7 and a 4.9 is both vague and hard to trace. A better approach is to break the big problem down into many small binary pass/fail checkpoints, and once you've accumulated enough of them, trace back upstream to find where things are actually going wrong.

He then turns to the education metaphor of a "rubric": like a math exam, you can't just check whether the final answer is correct — you also need to see whether the student "showed their complete work." A good student both gets the right answer and shows clear steps, which is really a bundle of multiple pass/fail conditions. The same applies to agents: you shouldn't just look at the output, you need to look at the chain of decisions in between. Here, beads function like requiring the agent to "show its work": each bead records the task it was working on at that point, its dependencies, its reasoning, and its notes. Later, during eval and error analysis, you can see directly which stage the thinking went wrong at, or which key premise pulled from the wrong data source, instead of only seeing the final one-line answer.

He also showed how he connects beads to Asana: his agents call themselves "fish," and each bead can be synced into a small Asana task, so that not just him but his colleagues too can assign work to agents directly through Asana. Conversely, agents can also use beads to "call on humans for help" — for instance, one time an agent noticed that another agent had a cowboy-hat avatar while it didn't, so it opened a "human task bead" asking him to help draw it a hat. This example shows how beads can also serve as a two-way coordination interface in which "a human is just another kind of tool."

In ongoing practice, he has agents keep using this structured workflow, and then has the agents turn around and "study themselves." For example, he took all the beads from the Atlantic hackathon as a dataset and asked the same group of agents to re-read how that project had been prepared, broken down, and successfully implemented. The agents ended up writing him a whitepaper, "Mise en place for agentic coding," analyzing the agentic development process through the lens of deliberate preparation. The paper was eventually accepted by an academic workshop on vibe coding and posted to arXiv; he is the official listed author, but most of the actual content was produced by the agents. To him, this shows that as long as the data (beads) is sufficiently structured, agents can not only write code but also take part in research and methodology-building about how they themselves work.

Finally, he distills all of this into a handful of principles borrowed from the classroom for evaluating and improving agent systems:

  1. Treat the project like a classroom and start with "backward design": clearly define what success looks like and what the final eval/exam looks like before you start building.

  2. Like a student writing in their homework planner every day, give humans and agents a shared "agenda" — here, a structured graph like beads, rather than scattered markdown notes.

  3. Teach through demonstration rather than piling up rules — provide plenty of positive and negative examples and a library of skills, so the agent can actually see what "good" looks like.

  4. Build a "show your work" rubric: don't just look at whether the answer is right — look at which tool calls it made along the way, what data it looked up, and which beads it created, so that eval points to concrete, improvable steps rather than an abstract score.

  5. Give the eval layer "back pressure": feed signal from failed tasks and pop-quiz-style random checks all the way back into prompts, skills, tool design, and system architecture, turning the whole agent system into a "self-correcting environment" rather than a passive log-recording observation system.

Overall, this talk uses a teacher's perspective to remind engineers: the best eval system isn't a stack of exam papers, but a "learning environment" that lets students (agents) keep learning, reflecting, and correcting themselves — and structured primitives like beads are one way of making that environment concrete inside agentic systems.
