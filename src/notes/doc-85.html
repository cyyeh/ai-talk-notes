The World Is Not Enough: RL's Environment Problem

Panel: Fleet, Prime Intellect, Taste

✕

This video is an AI Council panel titled "The World Is Not Enough: RL's Environment Problem," focused on one core question: to make RL / agents stronger, the real bottleneck is no longer compute — it's "high-quality environments."

Main points and conclusions

The moderator opens by noting that ML's bottleneck used to be training data; now it has shifted to how you obtain "environments" that are good enough and diverse enough for RL and post-training.

Several guests (the founders of Fleet, Prime Intellect, and Taste) first define "RL environment": in the traditional RL sense, it's a venue where an agent can take actions and receive observations and feedback; but they emphasize that today's environment types are already very diverse, including tasks with clear ground-truth answers, AI-as-a-judge (scored by a model), and even various evals/benchmarks — all of which can really be seen as a kind of environment.

They argue that the hardest part of environment design today isn't "generating questions" — it's "verification/scoring":
in verifiable domains like coding and math, checking an answer is easier than posing the question, so progress is fast; but once you move into subjective domains (design, aesthetics, creative work, writing style, personality, even scientific research), it becomes a matter of decomposing "fuzzy notions of good and bad" into an operable grading system — one that avoids reward hacking while still reflecting true quality and intent. This requires very deep domain expertise, so "grading/verification" itself becomes the new bottleneck.

They also criticize the fact that many "data/environment vendors" today claim to offer high-quality RL data, when in reality a lot of the environments and data aren't refined, and have limited real effect on capability improvement. Market consolidation is likely: most vendors will be dropped by labs for insufficient quality, and only a few that can reliably deliver environments that actually improve capability will survive.

What makes a "high-quality environment"?

The speakers answer "quality" from different angles:

  • Part of it is quantifiable: in verifiable domains (especially coding), a high-quality environment lets the model make significant progress on an independent eval/holdout test — the reward curve isn't just overfitting to the training questions themselves.

  • But in subjective domains, quality also includes: whether you're genuinely solving the "real problem" you want to solve, whether the environment is close to the real distribution, and whether you've avoided just feeling good about sitting at some local optimum. Many teams see a clean reward signal and conclude the environment must be good — but in reality they may have simply trained the model to be very good at ticking the "correct" box, rather than learning the right behavior.

  • They stress that eval and environment are tightly coupled: if the eval is designed too close to the training environment, you'll be misled into thinking you're doing great (the reward curve goes up), while generalization and real-world experience haven't actually improved. This is also why looking only at public benchmark scores (e.g. coding evals or SweetBench-style benchmarks) often fails to reflect real user preference and "taste."

Taste's founder uses design as an example:
judging "whether a website's design is good" involves basic instruction-following, layout, contrast, and font choice, but also creativity and fit with the target audience (e.g. a finance site vs. an avant-garde brand). Turning this fuzziness into a deterministic training signal usually requires a lot of upfront curation first: working with experts to organize the style space, mapping fonts and styles to audiences — only then do you have a shot at decomposing fuzzy aesthetics into evaluable subtasks that can drive large-scale environments and evals.

Human experts, taste, and the part that "can't be fully automated"

A recurring theme:
high-quality environments can't be separated from truly top-tier human experts, but experts are limited in number, and producing environments/annotations is itself expensive work.

The consensus is that, long term, things will move toward an architecture of "highly automated + human in the loop":

  • Use research and engineering techniques to turn existing data (e.g. production traces) into environments that can supply a reward signal, maximizing the automated share.

  • But wherever things are genuinely subjective, taste-driven, or governed by deep judgment calls, you still need a small but critical set of experts to define standards, provide seed signal, or correct systemic bias.
    They even point out that most users have neither the motivation nor the time to "train their aesthetic sense" — the average user's preference isn't necessarily the target you want. To learn "taste," you should specifically seek out taste-makers, rather than random crowd preference.

There's also an interesting tension here:
if you drive environment and design purely with hard metrics like A/B tests and conversion rate, you easily end up with something that "converts extremely well but looks awful"; if you optimize purely for the highest aesthetics or creativity, it may not serve business goals at all.
Their conclusion is closer to: explicitly model the "goal/intent," acknowledge that multiple equally valid answers exist, and then choose, based on the use case, which one you're optimizing for (mainstream appeal, refined niche aesthetics, conversion rate, long-term brand, etc.) rather than collapsing everything into a single metric.

Real-world data vs. synthetic environments: product and data flywheels

A large chunk of the middle section discusses how to turn real-world usage traces (production data) into RL environments and reward signal.

They argue that, going forward, every software company that wants a moat in the AI era will have to "redesign its product" to capture useful preference and behavioral data, forming its own proprietary data/model improvement loop.
Examples include:

  • Tesla's self-driving: when you disengage autopilot, the car asks you why — a clever way to solicit a reason label from the user at exactly the right moment, unobtrusive yet high-value.

  • Developer tools like Cursor: using an "accept/reject suggestion" UI so users provide preference signal through natural use.

  • Fintech or SaaS companies (like Ramp): deliberately designing features that make "user preference/judgment" explicit as events and labels — something that could not only improve their own models in the future, but potentially even become high-value RL data sellable to large labs.

They also note that if a company hasn't instrumented its product today to produce RL data, it will be in a very passive position later if it wants to build its own models or resist the general-purpose labs — it's likely to get "swallowed."
In the long run, every company will build out its own "research lab + environment-building" capability, the same way companies once built IT departments or ML teams.

That said, they also acknowledge production data has blind spots: for example, in the DoorDash case, what you observe is "someone ordered a salad, delivered in 20 minutes," but you don't know their real goal was "eat something healthy, quickly." Intent is hard to reconstruct purely from logs, so you still need deliberate product design and extra signal — you can't rely on raw usage traces alone.

Longer term: long-horizon tasks, continual learning, and automated environments

The later section on future trends makes several points:

  • The single biggest economic incentive will push massive resources toward agents that can "complete long-horizon tasks and directly substitute for knowledge work" — e.g. programming, law, finance — because they can deliver enormous productivity gains and business value.

  • Coding-style long-horizon environments will remain the most central category, because once you have good enough coding agents, you can indirectly automate any domain by "writing software for that domain" (e.g. writing a tax-filing system, automated contract review, etc.).

  • The real ceiling on capability will most likely only be broken by "deploying in the real world + harvesting production traces back," rather than endlessly self-congratulating inside a closed simulation — similar to how self-driving cars go from simulation to real road testing, then feed failure cases back into training.

  • Under this architecture, "environment-building" increasingly looks like blanketing real economic systems with sensors, observing agent-human interaction, then distilling those traces into a repeatable training environment.

At the same time, they note that environments shouldn't be seen purely as an RL-only tool. Environments can be used to produce high-quality trajectories for supervised fine-tuning, to study and classify model behavior, and to analyze safety risks (e.g. deceptive strategies learned during training) — uses that go well beyond "just the reinforcement learning step."

A few contrarian takes

In the closing quick-fire round, a few "contrarian" opinions worth noting:

  • Someone felt that rubric-style scoring is over-relied on, and actually constrains our ability to express quality and taste.

  • Vincent argues that simply rolling RL out to more economic domains, and continuing to scale RL, is enough on its own to drive us toward AGI — a large part of the model progress of the past few years already came from this kind of post-training/RL expansion.

  • Nic feels the term "RL environment" is itself somewhat misleading, causing people to tie environments too tightly to the RL technique specifically, while ignoring that an environment is really a much broader family of tools — for generating trajectories, analyzing behavior, and building a capability taxonomy — and shouldn't be confined to "reinforcement."

  • He also argues that, compared to a model that can run long, deep reasoning, a model that is "broadly trained across the world's distribution and can answer instantly" is what most people actually want.

Overall, the core message of this panel is:
if the previous generation's key asset was massive raw datasets, then in the "agents/RL era," the key asset becomes "environments" — scalable, verifiable, close to the real world, and able to encode human expertise and taste — along with the whole infrastructure needed to keep automating those environments and feeding them back into the product and model improvement loop.
