The Mythical Agent-Month

Posit

✕

This video is about "why software hasn't gotten better now that agents write the code," and tries to answer what a good engineer's value still is in a world where humans barely need to write code by hand anymore.

The speaker starts by saying he was actually a long-time AI skeptic, and it was only after using coding agents — going almost a year without genuinely "hand-writing" much code — that he began seriously thinking about what's still left for humans to do in software engineering. His conclusion: understanding the scope of a problem and its system, design and architecture skill, and "taste" — judging what makes software good — these three things are what matter, and always have. Typing out code by hand was always hard labor for humans; we just had no way to skip that "labor" before. Now that agents can write it for you, the genuinely hard part stands out even more.

He then borrows the classic concepts from Fred Brooks' The Mythical Man‑Month and "No Silver Bullet" to look at the "agent era":
Brooks splits complexity into "essential complexity" (the real problem the system has to solve, the core functionality needed, and the correct abstractions) and "accidental complexity" (the busywork that comes from languages, frameworks, build tools, CI, test frameworks, boilerplate, interacting with databases, and so on). Agents are extremely strong at handling accidental complexity: you can tell Claude or another model to refactor the entire test architecture, swap frameworks, cut boilerplate, or change languages, and they do it all quickly. But on essential complexity, they struggle to discern what actually matters, which domain model to use, how to layer things, or what the correct MVC/component logic behind a UI should be. If you don't hand them a clear design and abstraction up front, they'll produce a "matted hairball" of code — it runs, but it's loaded with technical debt, and every subsequent round of a new agent session just piles another slab of meat onto this Frankenstein's monster.

He compares this situation to the "tar pit" from The Mythical Man‑Month: it used to be masses of human engineers struggling in the tar; now it's a pile of parallel coding agents rampaging through different git worktrees, stepping on each other, causing endless rebases, conflicts, and rework. The only difference is that technical debt now accumulates at "machine speed" — problems that used to take a big system one or two years to develop can now show up in one or two weeks.

He also revisits Brooks' law — "adding people to a late project makes it later" — and the idea that a "small, elite surgical team beats a large team," which leads naturally into Conway's law: system architecture ends up mirroring organizational structure. A small team can maintain "conceptual integrity," making the whole system look like it came out of one head; a large team splits into many subsystems, which increases coordination cost and creates inconsistent boundaries. Now, if you throw a pile of agents at a large existing codebase without anyone responsible for maintaining the overall design, the result is, in practice, just as bad as it used to be with a large human team.

For greenfield projects, using an agent today to spin up a new repo, build the scaffold, and set up the build system feels amazing — it's got that "god's-eye terraform" rush to it; but he points out that once these projects go through a few hundred or a few thousand agent sessions and accumulate a few hundred thousand lines of code, they quickly turn into brownfield projects on their own. At that point, constrained by LLM context size and architectural limits, it becomes increasingly painful for agents to understand and refactor the whole system — even piling on design docs, a memory layer, and tool calls only slightly eases the underlying constraint.

This leads him to a key observation: those of us who used to write code as "individual contributors" are, in effect, all turning into software architects/product managers. Our main job has become "saying no": when an agent proposes some implementation or design direction, you have to be able to spot what's wrong with it and have the nerve to hit the brakes, demanding a more sensible abstraction and design instead. If you lack this kind of technical judgment and taste, and just keep saying yes, the system will rapidly turn, at the agent's high output rate, into an unmaintainable monster.

On the skills side, he talks about his own transformation: after 20-plus years of writing code, becoming fluent in Emacs, refactoring techniques, quickly navigating large codebases — traditional "hands-on" skills — almost none of it gets used now. What he thinks matters more going forward:
being able to establish "conceptual integrity" without writing much code, being able to clearly visualize the rough shape of a problem and its solution;
being able to "spell out" clearly to an agent, in the correct design vocabulary (architecture, abstraction, module boundaries, quality attributes), what you actually want;
being able to read and understand an existing system's design, and write useful specs and constraints based on that understanding.
So engineers and students will, going forward, need to spend more time reading code and studying architecture and design, rather than just practicing hand-writing coding problems.

He then talks about his actual "agentic engineering stack" and some tools he's built himself. On one hand, he makes heavy use of spec-driven development frameworks like Superpowers — first clarifying requirements, deciding on the design, and forming a detailed spec, then handing it off to a whole set of implementation agents to grind through. This workflow places human value at the "design and review" stage rather than the typing stage; he'll even kick off a plan that takes 14 hours to run, hand it to an agent to execute, go to sleep, and check the output the next morning.

On the other hand, he's also found that relying on these frameworks alone still produces code with plenty of bugs and quality issues, so he started building his own "automated code review and observability" system:
he mentions Robo Rev: every line of agent-generated code gets reviewed at least once, usually four or five times, cross-checked by different models and tools, with every review result going into a "ledger" that must be explicitly closed out — nothing disappears into some ephemeral conversation. Over a few months, he's run more than twenty thousand code reviews through this system.
He also built a session database that stores all his coding agents' sessions in one place, supporting full-text search and analysis of tool usage and token consumption, so he knows "where my money would actually be going if I were paying by the API."
Because the GitHub web interface is unstable and has a bad experience, he simply built his own GitHub UI (a middleman) — it uses GitHub's API but with an entirely different interface, and that's where he normally reviews PRs, merges, and reviews.
He also built his own issue tracker (Coda), storing issues and their state in SQLite, so agents can record their understanding of a problem and their progress within a structured system, instead of scattering markdown everywhere.
On top of that, he uses some parallel-agent management tools (like superset.sh, which he uses) to help manage twenty or thirty git worktrees at once, letting agents run linters, fix PRs, and so on for him.

On language choice, he says he made a clear pivot in early 2026: moving away from a Python-centric world — cutting Python almost entirely — to a Go-centric one instead. He wrote an article discussing "human ergonomics" versus "agent ergonomics":
Python is great for humans to read and write, which was also his motivation for building pandas back in the day;
but for agents, what matters more is whether compiling and testing are fast, whether deployment is easy (a static binary you can drop straight onto a server), and whether dependencies and the runtime carry overhead (something like the JVM looks heavier by comparison these days).
So languages like Go and Rust — which produce static binaries, compile fast, and deploy simply — turn out to have better "ergonomics" for agents. In a world where languages and frameworks are highly replaceable and get quickly rewritten by agents, a system's truly lasting value sinks down more into the lower-level, hard-to-replace core systems (things like database engines, DuckDB, and the like), rather than the choice of application-layer language on top.

Looking ahead, he acknowledges on one hand that models keep advancing rapidly — a step up every six months, agents doing more and more with fewer and fewer bugs, code correctness improving. On the other hand, he stresses that even so, essential design, scoping, and taste are problems that are hard for models to solve automatically. As things stand, the people who can truly push agents to the limit and produce high-quality software are still those engineers with deep technical background and architectural experience. He doesn't believe future models will fully replace these roles.

Finally, he brings all of this back to "taste and responsibility":
there's a huge amount of poor-quality, frustrating software out there in the world (he names Gmail's search experience directly), and behind it is often a product or design side that doesn't know what "good" looks like, and can't tell whether what they've built is actually bad.
In a world where "generating code is nearly free," the old excuse of "we don't have enough engineering resources" no longer holds up — the only problems left are really just two: you don't know what a good product looks like, or you can't recognize whether the thing in front of you is good or not.
He encourages people with design ability and taste to see this as an opportunity: look at all the terrible software left over from the pre-AI era, treat it as a target ripe for replacement, and use agents plus a new engineering stack to build a better version yourself.

For him personally, he's gone from a skeptical, veteran tool author to someone who's fully embraced "agentic engineering" — burning through a billion tokens a day, working seven days a week, an experimenter generating and reviewing huge volumes of code across multiple languages. He enjoys building tools while, at the same time, feeling his way toward new development workflows, and he hopes this talk reminds people that what truly shapes the quality of future software isn't who can type fastest, but who can maintain a system's conceptual integrity, design it properly, and have the courage to say "no" to an agent.
