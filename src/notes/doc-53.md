---
title: Judge the Judge: Building LLM Evaluators That Actually Work with GEPA
speaker: Mahmoud Mabrouk, Agenta AI
video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X4dEHRzBLmc
---
This talk demonstrates how to use GEPA to tune an LLM-as-a-judge until it aligns with human annotations and becomes genuinely trustworthy, for use in both offline and online evaluation.

The talk opens with a classic disaster story: you casually drop a "hallucination-detection LLM-as-a-judge" into your observability platform, the numbers all look great, but customers keep saying the system is terrible — because this judge was never actually calibrated against real human annotations. To avoid an evaluation that's "confidently wrong," what he wants to do is use an evolutionary optimization method like GEPA to tune the LLM's scoring prompt until it aligns closely with human annotations.

He then walks through one real case throughout the talk: an airline customer-service agent (from Sierra's TowBench dataset), which has many tools and very complex policy rules. He first preprocesses the originally rather complex assertion labels into "one human summary label per conversation trace" — for example, "non-compliant, because it canceled directly without first confirming whether the refund policy was met." The dataset is roughly 600 examples, with a reasonably OK ratio of compliant to non-compliant, but he repeatedly stresses: the data is genuinely messy and was generated in a complex way — a hard case, but exactly the kind that's suited to testing GEPA.

When designing evaluation metrics, instead of using one vague "success score," he breaks the problem down through error analysis into several explicit error types — e.g., policy adherence, response style, whether information was correctly conveyed to the customer, whether tools were called correctly, and so on. Importantly, every metric uses a binary judgment (yes/no), avoiding a fuzzy 1–5 scale as much as possible, because even two humans can rarely agree perfectly on that kind of scale, let alone get an LLM to learn it.

He then demonstrates the annotation workflow: a subject matter expert (here, a customer-service domain expert) reviews conversations one by one, first writing free-text comments, then classifying errors into the categories above, and crucially always writing down the "why" behind each judgment. This "reasoning text" turns out to be critical both for teaching the LLM-as-a-judge the policy, and for letting GEPA reflect on and revise the prompt later.

With the data and annotations in place, he moves into the core of GEPA: a prompt-optimization framework similar to a genetic algorithm. The flow: start with a seed prompt, run one round of eval, ask an LLM to produce a reflection on the poorly performing cases, and from that propose a new prompt (a "mutation") — sometimes merging two prompts into one (a "merge"). Each round scores many candidate prompts against a batch of test data, then uses logic similar to a "Pareto frontier" to select a set of candidates that are each "best at different cases," preserving diversity rather than just looking at the average score. New prompts are then sampled and generated from this candidate set, iterating round after round until the compute budget runs out.

He uses the GEPA author's open-source library, which has an API called "optimize_anything": you feed in a "candidate" (i.e., the current judge-prompt combination) and supply an evaluator function. What the evaluator does is run the LLM-as-a-judge with the current candidate prompt and log the output, error type, reasoning, and so on, so GEPA can produce good reflections and new candidates from this "diagnostic information."

On implementation details, he first hand-designed a seed judge that's deliberately "naive but clearly biased": default to "compliant" unless it sees a clear reason to call it "non-compliant." The point of this is to keep the LLM from judging wildly, guessing at policy purely on instinct. This initial judge scores around 61% accuracy on the validation set, saying "compliant" almost every time — but that's actually a sensible starting point: at least it doesn't pretend to be smart while actually judging at random.

He then shows how he wrote the reflection prompt. Using GEPA's default reflection prompt didn't yield effective policy rules, so he rewrote his own, explicitly telling the reflecting LLM: you're looking at an airline customer-service scenario, and you should use "the current judge's verdict + human ground truth + the human-written reasoning" to infer better policy clauses — adding or restructuring rules, turning vague statements into concrete conditions, and so on. This "reflection template with a strong prior" was one of the keys to the performance gain.

Next he demonstrates the whole pipeline in a Jupyter notebook: load the processed TowBench data, split into train/validation (with no overlapping cases), define the evaluator, and call GEPA to run a few hundred iterations. This experiment ran for several hours and burned through a fair amount of token spend. He repeatedly stresses: first check with a small number of iterations whether the reflections and new prompts make sense, observe the candidates GEPA produces and how they perform on individual cases, and only then gradually scale up the compute — rather than blindly throwing a huge compute budget at it from the start.

In terms of results, GEPA pulled the judge's accuracy from a little over 60% up to around 74% on validation; recall and precision for "catching non-compliance" in particular improved markedly — originally it could barely catch any non-compliance, and afterward it could distinguish reasonably well. On the training set, GEPA's Pareto frontier even reached 100% coverage, in the sense that for every single training case, at least one candidate prompt judged it correctly. However, compressing this knowledge — spread across many candidates — into "a single prompt that's highly accurate across every case" is still hard, and remains one of the current method's bottlenecks.

He also shares plenty of failed experiments and practical lessons. For instance: using small models (GPT-4o mini, other nano-tier or older models) as the judge or refiner failed almost completely — they couldn't learn the complex policy; what worked best was using a stronger model for reflection and a somewhat weaker — but not too weak — model for the judge. He also tried stuffing the full agent policy documentation directly into the seed prompt, which actually made optimization harder, likely because it got stuck early in a complex local optimum, making it harder for GEPA to explore new rule combinations; leaving the explicit policy out and learning purely from annotated reasoning instead left much more room to optimize.

Finally, he talks about cost and best practices: these experiments are expensive to run (a single experiment can cost $200–300), so choose your model, batch size, and iteration count carefully; a one-time prompt optimization using a strong model, in exchange for using a cheaper model long-term in online eval afterward, is an investment that can be amortized. His recommended workflow: first accept that "data and annotation design" is the hardest and most important step, then run small-scale experiments on GEPA to observe the quality of reflections and candidates, and scale up gradually from there; meanwhile, use tooling (he uses his own open-source platform, Agenta) to instrument both traces and the evaluation process, to make debugging and tuning easier.

Overall, the core message of this talk is: if you want LLM-as-a-judge to drive eval and automated optimization, you can't just write a nice-looking scoring prompt and ship it — you need to design clear error types and binary metrics for your specific business problem, collect human annotations with reasoning attached, then use an evolutionary framework like GEPA to systematically "calibrate" your judge against that data, while diligently debugging and tuning the model and templates throughout the process.
