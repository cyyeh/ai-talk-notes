How we made Trail of Bits AI-Native (so far)

Dan Guido, Trail of Bits

✕

This Trail of Bits talk at the AI Council is about how a security consulting firm actually became AI-native — not just handing employees ChatGPT accounts.

Dan Guido opens by explaining why most companies fail to use AI well: it's not a technology problem, it's a people problem. Most stay stuck at "AI-assisted": rolling out tools, drafting emails, shaving off a bit of time here and there — but company processes and organization never actually change. AI-native, by contrast, treats AI as a permanent teammate, assuming from the outset — in org design, knowledge management, and delivery process — that agents are working inside it.

He breaks down several psychological biases that make experts resist AI:
a sense of superiority (feeling that their own intuition and skill can't be replaced), identity threat (treating "security researcher" as a core identity, so hearing that a machine can "do it for you" feels threatening), distrust of black boxes (not trusting what they can't understand how the machine decided), and zero tolerance for machine error (one mistake from an algorithm and people stop trusting it for good). Trail of Bits' approach:
Using an "AI maturity matrix" so everyone can see clear levels and a progression ladder, forcing even people who think they're already strong to admit they might still be at Level 1;
Positioning AI as "amplifying expertise" rather than "replacing expertise" — asking senior engineers to turn their own techniques into reusable skills, so that when someone else hits a wall and uses that skill, they'll "remember it was you who wrote it," which actually reinforces the expert's standing;
Writing an AI usage handbook that clearly states which tools can be used, in which situations they can't, and why — reducing uncertainty;
Running internal hackathons instead of hard mandates, giving everyone two or three days to dive into a specific new tool or capability (like autonomous agents, sandboxing, guardrails) — forcing hands-on practice while preserving people's own initiative.

On the implementation side, he breaks Trail of Bits' "AI operating system" into several key components:
First, standardize the tool stack (e.g., company-wide standardization on Claude Code), which is the precondition for sharing configurations and building "verified-good" defaults — otherwise everyone does their own thing with zero leverage.
Write an AI handbook that clearly spells out: when client code must never be sent to a cloud model, which meeting-recording tools are banned, which situations require a local LLM, etc. — avoiding endless "is this OK to use?" debates on Slack, and ensuring consistent answers to clients.
Treat "being good at AI" as one of the professional skills, managed through a maturity matrix (from Level 0 resistance to AI, up to the highest level of designing and building new AI tools), folding AI capability into performance reviews and career growth paths — so the company doesn't split into "AI people" and "non-AI people" tribes.
Use hackathons as a management mechanism: each one targets a specific learning goal (e.g., bypassing a particular security mode, learning sandboxing and guardrails), with everyone experimenting in a safe zone (their own open-source repos); the point is "reps," not shipping flashy features, while pair programming ensures quality and promotes knowledge sharing.
Build "skills repositories": turn successful workflows into structured skills (with steps, examples, constraints, verification methods), produced in a public external version, an internal company version, and a "curated/vetted" version, so agents can call them directly and Trail of Bits gains external credibility.
For a secure supply chain, they built a "curated marketplace" for third-party skills, and set a "cooldown period" on the package managers across all dev machines: only packages published at least 7 days ago may be used, sidestepping most short-lived malicious packages — low cost, high security payoff.
Beyond that, they built a centralized repo that consolidates all "proven-good" AI configurations and examples (including personal Claude configs, agent templates, etc.); new hires just install this repo and follow it to quickly get onto the company's standard workflow, and the repo keeps getting updated as experience accumulates.
To support autonomous agents, they offer several sandbox options (devcontainer, macOS sandbox, and isolating agents via DropKit on DigitalOcean), without locking into a single approach — but making sure "everyone has an easy-to-adopt, secure sandbox."
Finally, they used MCP (Model Context Protocol) to wrap internal security tools like Slither into services agents can call, enabling unified permissions, auditing, and governance — moving toward something like Stripe's Minions model of "one MCP server managing hundreds of internal tools."

The result of this whole system: Trail of Bits now runs nearly a hundred plugins, 200-plus skills, dozens of specialized agents, and a large body of reference-knowledge files across its internal and external skill libraries, iterating across projects — skills span everything from sales proposals and government contract reports to engineering audits. On some suitable client projects, they've gone from finding an average of 15 bugs a week to around 200 — not by "speeding up a person" but by giving one engineer an entire team of specialized agents to scan the whole codebase in parallel and verify results. About 20 percent of the company's vulnerability findings are now first spotted by AI. In addition, because the sales team now uses AI for requirements definition, SOWs, and contract negotiation, the annual revenue quota each person can carry has jumped from the industry-standard $2-4M to $7-8M.

In the second half, he zooms out to the whole security industry. The key point: AI has made "finding vulnerabilities" very cheap; the real bottleneck has become "judging which vulnerabilities matter and which need fixing first." He cites some numbers: Mozilla used its own AI tooling to ship 271 security patches in one release (180 of them high severity); after years of honing his tools, curl's author was still able to dig up 200-300 defects in the past year alone; Pwn2Own was flooded with so many successful attack submissions that it had to close early. These aren't noise — they're real, verifiable CVEs and exploit chains. The problem isn't that "there's more debt" — it's that AI has suddenly shone a light on technical debt that already existed, creating a patch "liquidity crisis": enterprises can't absorb this volume of patching demand.

Trail of Bits now receives hundreds of bug packages a week from frontier labs, and even though these have already been screened internally by the labs, in practice they still find: roughly 10 percent can't be reproduced, 10 percent are false positives, 20 percent have already been fixed upstream, and quite a few get downgraded by them from critical to medium. To cope, they built a "mechanical review layer": automatically running false-positive checks on these bugs (pulling the latest code, replaying the PoC, checking upstream patches, deduplicating), at an average cost of about $5 per bug, as a first "mechanical screening" gate. But what really takes time and needs high-value human judgment is: is it exploitable in the actual deployment environment? How does it relate to the overall threat model? Can it be chained with other weaknesses into a viable attack path? Current large models — even the newest, like Opus — still seriously overestimate severity here, and can't reliably assess exploitability. So as "discovery" and "mechanical verification" get automated, the scarcest resource becomes human security judgment time.

He goes on to discuss the impact on professional services. Drawing on a view from the legal industry, he argues the traditional partnership model built around billable human hours will gradually disappear, replaced by a "neo firm" that's half practicing experts, half engineering team, treating innovation and tool-building as a core competency. This looks more like the forward-deployed engineer model: people who understand the client's business context work on the front line, backed by dedicated agents and tooling. The number of organizations writing software has exploded, and security consultants now need to get up close with every highly specialized system in order to give a meaningful security assessment. You can see the same signal in where capital is flowing: OpenAI and Anthropic do have their own forward-deployed engineers, but not nearly enough, so they've formed alliances with dozens of service firms — because you still need a lot of human effort to help customers adopt and run these AI capabilities. That fact alone is evidence that AGI hasn't arrived yet.

On the technology roadmap, he predicts "private inference" will become the norm. We're already seeing things like Meta's "invisible chat," built with AMD SEV-SNP confidential computing, where in theory even the server operator can't see the prompts or responses. Systems like this claim to be unbreakable "even by a party with access to all the hardware," which is extremely hard to implement — Trail of Bits has been brought in to do deep pre-launch reviews for products like WhatsApp. The conclusion: this stuff can be made secure, but it requires very solid system-level protections and audit work.

Finally, he sums up a "minimum viable AI-native work-design path": standardize tooling, write a clear AI handbook, build a maturity matrix, drive learning through hackathons, distill outcomes into a skills repo, build a secure sandbox and tooling governance layer, then move on to private inference and more advanced deployment. The key point: this whole thing is a system — you can't just copy one or two pieces and expect the same results. And the big open questions include: how do you sell services to an AI once AI itself becomes the one placing orders on behalf of customers; how do you defend against new attacks like prompt injection in a heavily agentic environment; and how do you keep learning, continuously, from company-wide daily AI usage, updating policy and capability every day at "agent speed."
