The End of the Internet As We Know It

Raffi Krikorian, Mozilla

✕

This video is an interview with Mozilla CTO Raffi Krikorian at AI Council, centered on what he wrote in a New York Times op-ed: "the truce on internet security is over."

He first explains what the "truce" was: in the past, writing software was hard, and finding vulnerabilities was also hard — the difficulty on both sides was roughly comparable, so even though there were plenty of bugs, they were exploited at scale relatively slowly, creating a kind of time-lag safety buffer. But once models like Anthropic's Mythos appeared, they simultaneously made "writing code easier" and "finding zero-day vulnerabilities easier," and that balance broke. Critical open-source components (ffmpeg, OpenSSL, curl, etc.) often have only one or two maintainers, yet they underpin the entire internet's infrastructure — in a world of AI-automated vulnerability scanning, they're drastically under-resourced, which effectively exposes the whole network to risk at once.

He disagrees with the optimistic view that "AI will fix AI itself, so there's not much to worry about," because the sequencing is inverted: mass discovery and exploitation of vulnerabilities comes first, and patching and hardening only catch up later — it's that long stretch in between, the "vulnerable window while things are wide open," that's the truly dangerous part.

He then turns to the shift in how software gets developed: AI is no longer just a tool, but will increasingly become a "co-author." In practice, engineers already treat the model as a teammate, giving a 400-line AI-generated diff only a quick skim, with the real attention going to tests and behavior. So he argues development culture and tooling need to change: merges should rely more on "behavior and evals" rather than just reviewing the raw diff, and git itself should evolve to record "prompts, model version, eval results, and provenance," so we know which model, in what training state, produced which change.

On engineering management and organizational culture, he uses Mozilla as an example: the Firefox team is very conservative, treating AI as an assistive tool and letting only senior engineers touch critical core code; the Mozilla AI team sits at the opposite extreme, with entire codebases produced almost entirely by models. He's deliberately tolerating this "multi-track experimentation" and some amount of mess for now, relying on a regular technical council to share experience and gradually converge on best practices. He also suggests the industry treat models as "software interns" — imposing boundaries on them, forbidding them from touching critical sections, and requiring clear version and provenance records.

Discussing security and business models, he criticizes how many SaaS companies today treat security capabilities like SSO as a "paid premium feature," arguing security should be the default, not a premium. He advocates moving toward "secure-by-design" — for example, more aggressively adopting languages like Rust, and thinking through how to migrate legacy systems to safer architectures. Open-source maintainers are critical infrastructure here, and he compares them to "essential workers" during the pandemic, arguing that large AI/cloud companies should funnel real money and engineering time back to them, or the whole ecosystem's foundation will collapse.

He then discusses the economics and philosophy of open source and open models:
historically, projects like Linux and various open-source projects in the ecosystem relied on a model where companies "serve themselves while also giving back to the community" — partly for recruiting, partly for marketing and culture, and partly genuine technical reciprocity. He believes the model era needs something similar but taken further, because open weights alone aren't enough — you also need transparent data, evaluation, and post-training processes, otherwise there's no way to understand these models' ethical or decision-making leanings (for instance, he mentions research finding that Western and Chinese models give completely different answers on kidney-transplant decisions).

He also emphasizes that "open doesn't need to conquer 100% of the market" — what matters is having a trustworthy, genuinely usable open alternative in existence. The mere existence of Firefox/Signal forces other browsers and communication protocols to raise their privacy and encryption standards. Likewise, if competitive open AI models and applications exist, they can put pressure on the big platforms, pushing the whole industry in a direction more favorable to users — even though most people will never actually look at the source code.

On large-model interfaces, he points out that the shift from "Google's ten blue links" to "ChatGPT giving you one answer" is hugely significant. Users used to roughly understand what they were choosing between; now it's much easier to hand decision-making entirely over to the model — and the model has commercial incentives behind it (for instance, research finds many answers skew toward platform-sponsored results), which represents a new kind of risk to human autonomy and to the structure of our information diet.

He holds cautious optimism about "whether open models can stand on their own economically": currently, some of the best open-weight models are largely sponsored by big companies, at enormous cost. Companies releasing open-source projects in the past often had recruiting and community-building motives, and that motivation should still exist for models going forward — but it will take more innovative business structures and full transparency of data/models before this can genuinely become a viable alternative to closed models.

Taking the longer view, he doesn't want to see "seven AGIs" — he wants to see "seven billion AGIs": everyone running an agent on their own device that's aligned with their personal values and data, without that data needing to be centralized with a single big company. Achieving this requires solving the developer-experience problem for open models (a default setup as good to use as "the MacBook of the AI world"), breaking the compute monopoly (escaping a single GPU vendor and a closed software stack), and building fine-grained mechanisms for data authorization and provenance.

Later he talks about talent and education. He predicts that by 2035 there will be far more "builders" than there are today: through "vibe coding," people without an engineering background will also be able to build systems; his own 10-year-old son is an example — using an AI IDE to make a bunch of small games, and then, in turn, becoming motivated to learn a real programming language to understand what's underneath. He believes we'll end up redefining the difference between "engineer" and "builder," with experience and taste becoming more central to engineering value: people who understand architecture and have learned from past failures will still have a huge edge when collaborating with models, because they're better at judging what output "looks right but will actually blow up."

At the same time, he worries that if everything becomes too "frictionless," it will erode the "productive struggle" that learning requires. Learning has always been about building judgment through making mistakes, correcting them, and accumulating scars, whereas today's tools tend to pave over every pothole for you. He feels the next real battleground is education: figuring out how to let kids make full use of AI while still preserving enough challenge and frustration for them to learn to think and take responsibility, rather than becoming completely dependent on whatever single answer the system hands them.

Overall, this conversation is, on one hand, a warning that "AI has thrown the internet security equation out of balance," and, on the other, a call to action: re-fund open-source maintainers, design new engineering workflows that treat AI as a team member, build genuinely usable open models and toolchains, and start now designing a "some friction, but real growth" way of using AI for the next generation — so we don't end up handing too much control over to a handful of platforms and black-box models before we've even figured out what's happening.
