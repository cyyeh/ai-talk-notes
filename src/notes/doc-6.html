AI: too good to be true, too bad to be useful

Diogo (formerly OpenAI), TypeSafe AI

✕

This talk is about why today's LLM/AI products create such a striking gap between seeming "incredibly impressive" and actually "not being all that useful."

Speaker Diogo (formerly at OpenAI, a co-author of ChatGPT and RLHF) uses his experience at OpenAI as background and points to a core paradox: across every kind of benchmark, model capability keeps climbing, looking like it's tracing an exponential curve, yet society has barely seen any corresponding revolutionary change in real economic productivity or broad-based automation. His key question: "if the models are really this strong, where did the economic revolution go?"

His answer: nearly every mainstream LLM today has been optimized for "human assistance" rather than "automation." In other words, they're trained to produce strings of text that "look good to a human, sound coherent, and come across as smart" — not to reliably and predictably get things right in the real world. As a result:

  • In "a human is watching it get used" scenarios (writing assistance, drafting customer-support replies, code suggestions, decision explanations a human will check), LLMs look "incredibly useful, even seemingly superhuman."

  • The moment you need the model to "make its own decisions, take its own actions, and own the risk" — say, truly automating a customer-support flow, handling money movements, full self-driving, or writing code straight into production with nobody checking — reliability falls badly short, so almost nobody is actually willing to let it run fully autonomously.

He calls this difference the "human in the loop vs. machine in the loop" distinction: today's successful applications are, at their core, all cases where "the human stays in the loop, AI acts as the assistant," while the failures or the overhyped cases are mostly attempts to "let AI take over the task entirely."

He then explains why this happens, from the angle of optimization and RLHF. RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback) is an almost mandatory step before shipping any mainstream LLM, used to make the model "more like what people like to see." But this kind of training causes "mode dropping": the model tends to pick the one output path that "looks safest and most reasonable," sacrificing every other possible-but-less-"pleasing" path along the way. The result is:

  • Answers almost always look "reasonable, logical, very fluent."

  • But in long chains of reasoning, sequential decision-making, or scenarios demanding high precision, small errors accumulate, and reliability ultimately collapses fast.

  • So people start saying "LLMs are more like people than like traditional software — hard to predict, and you can't get true n-nines reliability out of them." But to him, this actually runs against the "highly controllable, high-precision" standard machine learning has always pursued in every other domain.

He draws a comparison between the drive-thru and software engineering: if even the drive-thru — a scenario that's process-driven, clearly structured, and backed by a huge economic incentive — still hasn't been successfully automated by LLMs, then why would anyone believe that "the whole of software engineering" could be swallowed whole by agents in one go? He points out that today's "AI customer service" and "AI agent" systems almost all have a human backstopping them: the model can chat with you and look things up in documents, but for actions that actually touch accounts, orders, or permissions, the system mostly won't let the model act directly.

He also criticizes an industry narrative along the lines of "it's your company's technology that isn't good enough": when a company finds AI automation isn't working well internally, it's easy to assume the problem is your own "lack of technical strength" or "lack of AI experts," so you bring in more consultants or vendors to help. But in his view, based on what he's actually seen at top labs and portfolio companies, it looks far more like the entire technical approach simply isn't suited to genuinely high-risk automation.

He then moves to the higher-level question of "what are you actually optimizing for." He reinterprets the so-called "bitter lesson," arguing that the truly important order of priority is: pick the right task first, then data, and only then compute. Many people take away "compute matters more than algorithms," but in his view, the reason Anthropic has caught up to, or even closed in on, OpenAI in certain capabilities is that it has a clearer "optimization target" (for example, designing more explicitly around reasoning and tool use) — not some sudden overtake in compute or data scale.

He further points out that trying to make a model both "superhuman at pleasing humans (assistant)" and "superhuman reliable (automation)" at the same time is essentially pulling against two conflicting optimization directions, which leaves the model's behavior sitting on a "jagged, uneven frontier" — it neither achieves true automation-grade reliability, nor keeps the full conversational experience intact. You can't "train two extreme traits at once with the same loss function"; at best you get a compromise, and you never reach the extreme of either end.

There's a whole stretch where he talks about "the consequences for the world":
Because the current state of LLMs is "looks smart, is actually unreliable," product design tends to offload the risk onto the user (for example, an AI customer-service bot says the wrong thing or gives bad directions, while real authority stays with a human behind the scenes; or AI fills every corner of the world with junk content and marketing copy). The user experience ends up becoming "I'm forced to deal with something that half-understands me and stands between me and a real person / a real system," and over time that naturally breeds a resentment toward "AI" — except that resentment is really a reaction to "bad product design" and "the wrong optimization target."

In the second half, he turns to "type-safe language models" and TypeSafe AI, the company he founded — but the point isn't really to pitch it, it's to lay out the direction he believes in: if we're going to genuinely move toward automation, LLMs have to "go beyond pure text input/output" and become truly, deeply integrated with programming languages, type systems, and structured data — turning into a "basic computing primitive" like a database or an API, rather than forever playing the role of a "talking stand-in for a coworker." He believes this path is difficult, but it's the only way to reach the vision — one many people have already given up on — of "AI driving structural economic change."

He also looks back on his early experience with RLHF: once GPT‑3.5 had RLHF applied to it, the model already clearly surpassed most human teams at "following instructions and writing fluent copy." Internally at the time, there was even discussion of "whether this counted as some form of AGI." But once it actually ran in the market, the bulk of real-world usage ended up concentrated in copywriting, sales-pitch emails, and other applications of questionable marginal value — not the kind of "large-scale automation of real tasks" he had hoped for. This experience made him reflect: the problem isn't that "the model still isn't smart enough" — it's that we defined the task wrong from the start, putting the optimization target on "making people feel good" instead of "getting the work right."

His closing summary is roughly this:

  • Today's LLMs are optimized to be "assistants" rather than "doers," and that directly causes the gap between "looking absolutely magical in the news" and "almost no real automation in practice."

  • Under the current technical approach, letting an LLM "make its own decisions" in scenarios with real-world risk is dangerous and irresponsible — it will hurt users and damage the reputation of the entire AI field.

  • But that doesn't mean AI's potential has already peaked — it means we need new training objectives and system designs that let models actually drive automation safely and reliably, instead of only producing "text that looks smart."

The emotional register of the whole talk feels a bit like "reflection + confession + manifesto": on one hand, he acknowledges that as a co-author of RLHF and ChatGPT, he bears some responsibility for the current path; on the other hand, he's also trying to convince the audience not to treat today's form of LLM as "the endpoint of AI," but to see it as a side branch — the real prize still lies in redesigning AI systems capable of driving genuine automation and economic transformation.
