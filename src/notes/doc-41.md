---
title: From Prediction to Uplift: Causal Modeling for Better Decisions

This Intuit talk is about why building prediction models alone doesn't necessarily lead to good decisions, and how uplift (causal) modeling fills that gap.

The speaker starts with a familiar example: predicting customer churn or purchases. Traditional prediction models answer "who will churn / who will buy" — for instance, finding that "customers who call support churn at 3x the rate of those who don't," with an F1 of 0.9, which looks great. But if you act on that by deciding to "hide the support contact option to prevent calls," the result might be that customers who were already unhappy and about to churn simply lose their last chance to be retained — you've actually made churn worse. The key question is: does calling support cause churn, or do customers who are about to churn call support? That's the difference between prediction and causation.

Next he illustrates this with an email marketing campaign: you might observe that people who received the email have a 20% purchase rate, versus 8% for those who didn't, suggesting a 12-point uplift. But if the marketing team only targeted customers who were already active and likely to buy, that 12 points is actually a mix of two things: the real email effect, plus selection bias from "there being more people who would have bought anyway." What we actually want to know is the portion that's extra because of the email.

Ideally, the gold standard for this kind of causal question is a randomized controlled trial (A/B test): randomly assign customers to treatment (receive the email) or control (don't), so the two groups are statistically identical on every feature — then the average difference between the groups is the true effect of the email. But A/B tests are expensive, take time, and can even be unethical in some settings (e.g., healthcare) — and a lot of data is only observed after the fact. Uplift modeling is about using a model, in these imperfect settings, to estimate the causal effect of an intervention on a given group of people.

He then introduces the conceptual foundation of uplift. For each individual i, there are two potential outcomes: the outcome without treatment, ‎⁠Y_i(0)⁠, and the outcome with treatment, ‎⁠Y_i(1)⁠. The individual treatment effect (ITE) is ‎⁠Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)⁠. If you could observe both worlds, you could perfectly decide who's worth targeting:\
– People who'd buy either way are "sure things" — don't waste resources on them;\
– People who'd never buy no matter what are "lost causes" — no amount of outreach helps;\
– People who only buy if they get the email are "persuadables" — these are the people we're actually looking for;\
– People who would have bought but are put off by the outreach are "sleeping dogs" — the intervention has a negative effect on them.

The problem is, we can only ever observe one of these two worlds (with treatment or without) — the other is an unobservable "counterfactual." So uplift models don't estimate ITE directly; instead they estimate the "conditional average treatment effect" (CATE): ‎⁠E[Y(1) - Y(0) | X]⁠ over a group of people with similar features X. Put simply, it's "the average uplift for people who look like this." The model outputs a predicted uplift for each person, which in practice becomes a ranked list from "most worth targeting" to "least worth targeting" — you target as far down the list as your budget allows.

On the implementation side, he introduces using meta‑learners for uplift:\
The S‑learner is the simplest kind: treat the treatment variable (e.g., whether the email was sent) as just another feature, train a single model, then make two predictions on the same X — once with treatment=1 and once with treatment=0 — and the difference between them is the uplift. Its advantages are that it's easy to get started with and works even with little data; its downside is that the model may treat treatment as just an "ordinary feature," and in tree models it may not even split on it at an important position, so the effect ends up poorly captured.

The T‑learner, on the other hand, trains a separate model for treatment=1 and treatment=0, with each side only seeing its own data and not interfering with the other. At prediction time, the treatment model estimates ‎⁠Y(1)⁠ and the control model estimates ‎⁠Y(0)⁠, and the difference is the uplift. The benefit is that each side can learn different patterns and even use different model types (e.g., a neural net on one side, a tree model on the other); the downside is it's more data-hungry, since you're effectively splitting your data in half to train.

The hard part is evaluation. Standard ML metrics (accuracy, F1, AUC) only measure how accurately you predict "who will buy," whereas uplift needs to measure "who will change their behavior because of the treatment." And since there's no ground-truth individual uplift label, you can't compute "uplift accuracy." He shares a practical approach that works well: rank customers by the model's uplift score from high to low, split them into equal-sized buckets (e.g., 10 deciles), and in each bucket compute the average outcome difference between treatment and control (the observed uplift). If the model is actually useful, the buckets on the left (high predicted uplift) should show the highest actual uplift, decreasing as you move right — the middle-to-tail buckets might even show negative uplift (the sleeping-dogs zone). If this uplift curve is roughly monotonically decreasing, and the first few buckets show clearly positive uplift, he'd consider the model worth shipping. In practice, you also need to decide "how deep to target": you can choose to target only the top few buckets (high uplift, high ROI), or target everyone with positive predicted uplift (broader coverage but lower average uplift), depending on the cost of the intervention. For example, if the email is nearly free, you can target everyone with uplift>0; if it's a $50 retention offer, you'd only target the front segment where the uplift is large enough to offset the cost.

Finally, he sums up when to use uplift: when the treatment has a cost, and you either can't or don't want to treat everyone the same way, you should shift from predicting "who will do X" to predicting "who will change their behavior because we did something." If the treatment is nearly free and can be given to everyone, a traditional prediction model is already enough. In practice, he recommends teams start with an S‑learner, and try a T‑learner once there's enough data — the key is making sure each treatment/control arm has enough samples, and validating with an uplift‑by‑bin curve. In the Python ecosystem, packages like causalML, scikit‑uplift, and econML are good starting points, though the ecosystem and tutorials still aren't as mature as scikit‑learn's.
